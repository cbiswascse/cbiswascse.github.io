A career that started with a puzzle

Long before I wrote my first line of production code, I was the kid who couldn't put a puzzle down.

Jigsaws, riddles, logic grids, the last stubborn corner of a Rubik's cube — I loved the quiet thrill of staring at something tangled and slowly watching it click into place. There's a very particular kind of joy in that moment when scattered pieces suddenly become a clear picture. What I didn't realize back then was that I was training for a career.

When I discovered computer science, it felt like someone had handed me an infinite box of puzzles. Every algorithm was a riddle, every bug a mystery with a hidden cause, every system a structure waiting to be understood and improved. The problem-solving instinct that made me love games was exactly the instinct that great software engineering rewards. I was hooked — and I still am.

That curiosity carried me from a Bachelor's in Computer Science in Chittagong, through a Master's in Information Technology in Frankfurt, into research roles in network security and railway systems, and finally into engineering teams where I get to solve real problems for real users every single day. Today I work with Ruby on Rails and React, lean into Domain-Driven Design and Event Sourcing, and care deeply about writing code that other people can read, trust, and build on.

At the heart of it, I'm still that same puzzle-solver — just with a bigger box, better tools, and a team to solve alongside.
